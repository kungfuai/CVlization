## Findings (2026-04-12)

### What is reliably established

These findings are based on properly sized evaluations (n≥50) or consistent
results across multiple independent experiments.

1. **MXC format is necessary.** 0% pitch accuracy with XML targets across all
   6 models tested. XML tokens are consumed by preamble before reaching notes.
   This is replicated across every model and training configuration.

2. **Models under 8B cannot learn pitch.** Four different 3-4B architectures
   (Ministral-3, Gemma-3 4B, DeepSeek-OCR-2) all score 0-1% pitch. This is
   consistent across LoRA ranks (r=8, r=32) and training durations (2-4 epochs).

3. **Architecture matters more than scale.** Qwen3.5-9B (9.5B params) outperforms
   Qwen3-VL-32B (32B params) — 35% vs 23% pitched-only similarity. Both measured
   on n=10, but the gap is large enough to be directionally reliable.

4. **LoRA r=32 is optimal for Qwen3.5-9B.** r=16 (33%), r=32 (36%), r=64 (32%)
   on n=10. r=64 overfits without improving. The rank sweep was done on the same
   data with identical settings.

5. **Training target truncation was a major bottleneck.** Level 7 MXC is ~4200
   tokens. At `max_length=4096`, the training target and EOS token were
   truncated. Increasing to 8192 improved pitch from 47% → 67% (+20pp).
   EOS is correctly learned on Levels 1-3 (where targets fit within 4096)
   but not learned when targets are truncated.

6. **Curriculum learning eliminates training instability on long sequences.**
   Training from scratch on 24-measure content produces 7-16 loss spikes
   (regardless of quantization: 4-bit or bf16). Resuming from a 16-measure
   adapter eliminates ALL spikes (0 in 3000 steps) and achieves 5× lower
   eval_loss. See Experiment 16.

7. **bf16 quantization does NOT help Qwen3.5 OMR fine-tuning.** Despite
   Unsloth's recommendation, bf16 produces similar spikes and slightly
   worse accuracy (64% vs 68% pitched-only) compared to 4-bit. See
   Experiment 16A.

8. **Loss spikes correlate with output sequence length.** 16-measure runs
   have 0-2 spikes; 24-measure runs have 7-16 spikes. This is a known
   issue in LLM fine-tuning ("Spike No More", COLM 2025). The curriculum
   fix addresses the root cause.

9. **Level 6 (16m) reliably scores 95% with either 4K or 8K context.** The
   8K control experiment (Experiment 14) reproduced the original Level 6
   result with n=50 validation.

10. ~~**The voice part is the dominant accuracy factor (−55pp).**~~ **RETRACTED.**
    With stable training + curriculum, the voice part penalty is small
    (95% → 91% = −4pp at 16m; 94% → 82%\* = −12pp at 24m).
    \* confounded with lyrics — see #11.

11. **Lyrics penalty is only ~4pp (not ~27pp).** With curriculum (Exp 18):
    7a (91%) → 7b curriculum (87%) = −4pp at 16m. The from-scratch −27pp
    was almost entirely training dynamics. Curriculum also fixes
    overgeneration (coverage 100%) and parts accuracy (50/50).

12. **More synthetic data lowers eval_loss but does not improve pitch.** 1K
    → 5K samples: eval_loss improved 5× (0.002 → 0.0004), but pitch stayed
    ~47% on n=50 (before the 8K context fix).

13. **Inference speed: ~26 tok/s, ~2.5 min/sample for Level 7.** Consistent
    across runs on Blackwell RTX PRO 6000.

**Key observations:**

1. **Curriculum learning is the definitive fix for long-sequence instability.**
   Level 6b (24m) with curriculum (resume from 16m adapter) scores **94%**
   pitched-only — matching Level 6 (16m) at 95%. Zero training spikes.

2. **The "length penalty" was almost entirely training instability.** The
   from-scratch 68% score made it look like 24m content was inherently
   ~27pp harder. With curriculum, the penalty is only 1pp.

3. **bf16 does not help.** Despite Unsloth's recommendation, bf16 produces
   similar spikes (7 vs 9) and slightly worse accuracy (64% vs 68%).

4. **Openscore accuracy is 18% pitched-only on n=50.** Much worse than the
   old n=10 estimate (35%). The synthetic-to-real gap is 76pp (94% → 18%).
   Real music complexity — not training dynamics — is the bottleneck.

5. **eval_loss is misleading** — it tracks format/syntax memorization, not
   actual transcription accuracy. Can coexist with degenerate generation.

### Curriculum eliminates the length penalty

With curriculum (train 16m first, then resume on 24m):

| | 16 measures | 24m from-scratch stable | 24m curriculum |
|---|---|---|---|
| Piano only | 95% | 68% (−27pp) | **94% (−1pp)** |
| Voice+piano+lyrics | 64% (7b stable) | 67% (old Level 7) | **82% (7a→7)** |

The length penalty on piano-only was entirely training instability.
For voice+piano+lyrics, curriculum improves from 67% → 82% (+15pp).

### All apparent penalties were mostly training dynamics

With curriculum applied everywhere, the real penalties are small:

| Factor | Without curriculum | With curriculum | Actual penalty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Length (16→24m) | −27pp | **−1pp** | ~1pp |
| Voice part (2→3 parts) | −55pp\* | **−4pp** | ~4pp |
| Lyrics | −27pp | **−4pp** | ~4pp |
| All combined | — | **−13pp** | ~13pp |

\* Original claim was retracted — caused by training instability on 6b.

Curriculum also fixes overgeneration (coverage 100%) and parts accuracy
(50/50 on all curriculum runs). The earlier "lyrics cause overgeneration"
was a training dynamics artifact.

### Loss spikes scale with output sequence length (verified)

| Run | Output tokens | Spikes >5× | Max ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| 16-measure runs | ~2000-2700 | 0-2 | up to 12× |
| 24-measure from-scratch | ~3000-4200 | 7-16 | up to 88× |
| **24-measure curriculum** | ~3000 | **0** | — |

Curriculum is the canonical fix. Lower LR + tighter clipping is a
partial mitigation (reduces but doesn't eliminate spikes). bf16 doesn't
help (similar spikes, slightly worse accuracy).

### The synthetic-to-real gap

| Dataset | Pitched-only | n | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 7 (curriculum) | **82%** | 50 | Synthetic voice+piano+lyrics |
| Level 8 (curriculum) | **77%** | 50 | +format features |
| Level 9 (curriculum) | **65%** | 50 | +multi-voice |
| Openscore (from Level 7) | 18% | 50 | No format features |
| Openscore (from Level 9) | **23%** | 50 | With all format features |

**The synthetic ladder improved openscore by +5pp (18% → 23%)** via
Levels 8-9 (format features + multi-voice). But the gain is modest
relative to the remaining 77pp gap.

Both synthetic and openscore use the same LilyPond renderer — there is
NO visual domain gap. The model reads the notation fine; it hasn't
learned the musical vocabulary of real compositions.

### Why the synthetic ladder hit diminishing returns

Each synthetic level adds format complexity that makes the synthetic task
harder (82% → 77% → 65%) but transfers only marginally to openscore.
The model spends capacity learning format constructs rather than music
reading. The remaining gap is **content complexity**:

1. **Page density**: real pages have 4-5× more notes than synthetic
2. **Real harmonic language**: chromatic progressions, modulations,
   suspensions — synthetic uses random pitch walks
3. **Voice leading**: real inner voices follow musical rules; synthetic
   voices are independent random melodies
4. **Varied density**: real music alternates sparse/dense textures;
   synthetic has uniform density throughout
5. **Token budget**: even at 16K, 15% of pages are truncated

### Open questions

1. **Would training directly on openscore (with Level 9 adapter) work
   better?** The synthetic ladder taught MXC format; now the model needs
   real music content. The Level 9 adapter is the best starting point.

2. **Is RL viable at 23% openscore?** Marginal — the model produces some
   valid structure but too many errors for reliable reward signals. SFT
   on real data should come first.

3. **Would a larger base model help?** Qwen3.5-9B may lack capacity for
   the full openscore task. A 32B model with the same curriculum could
   score significantly higher.

4. **Should we simplify the MXC target?** The full MXC format has many
   features (directions, headers, lyrics) that inflate token count but
   don't contribute to pitch accuracy. A pitch-only MXC variant could
   let the model focus on what matters.

## Next steps

### 1. Key signature training

The accidental errors suggest the model needs more examples of each key signature.
Current Level 3 has random keys (-4 to +4) across 800 samples = ~100 per key.
Generating more samples or oversampling rare keys may help.

### 2. RL with musical correctness rewards

Use structural rewards (valid MXC, measure durations, pitch range) to push
past the SFT ceiling. Best attempted after diagnostic runs stabilize.

### 3. Investigate bimodal accuracy distribution

The 50-sample eval shows 46% of samples >50% pitch and 26% near 0%.
Understanding what distinguishes success vs failure cases would guide
data augmentation or curriculum design.

### 4. vLLM integration for faster eval

vLLM v0.17.0 has native Qwen3.5 support with optimized linear attention
kernels. Once tokenizer compatibility is resolved, this could enable
batched inference for 2-4× faster evaluation.
